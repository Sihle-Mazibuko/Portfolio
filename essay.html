Essay

Superbalist (https://superbalist.com/)

by Sihle Mazibuko

Superbalist is a South African online retailer with a focus mostly on fashion and accessories but also deals with décor and electronics. Its website follows the same trend in terms of various content that all work together to form this working website. In this essay, the Superbalist website shall be analysed by its design aspects, factoring in its user interface elements and the user experience created from interacting and analysing the interactive design ethics found within the website to critique the website in its entirety. The content and information discussed are accurate as of the 2nd of May 2023, information found may be subject to change upon viewing the site at a later stage.

Having well-executed user interface elements enables the interface of the website to be easily interpreted and beautiful. The home page to contains a somewhat logical flow. The viewer’s eyes to some extent always scroll down as information scrolls up however this is not always constant as at some point your eyes are to move across the page or even stay static to allow the change in the flow of information. Superbalist counters this problem by displaying clear elements that provide legible and readable information. They achieve this by using serif fonts (specifically sans serif) and this is to ensure the user can read the words on the screen. This strictness in font does change to a combination of Arial (a font deemed to be highly legible) and sans-serif in the checkout pages as this part of the website is crucial for the user to be able to read and input the correct information. However, they seem to neglect this clarity of information with the use of colour. Fashion is an industry of loud and robust expression however in some cases (such as website design) it is important to be aware that many people, of different backgrounds and knowledge, will have to view and interpret your website. Having an overload of colours on the homepage can easily draw many people away as there is no synchronicity in the elements displayed. They do rectify this by switching to white backgrounds on products (once you start viewing them) however this effort can be too little too late as most consumers will not even reach the point of seeing a more balanced colour scheme due to the home page’s complexities. The text colour choice however in these crucial screens is concerning as the use of a light grey on a white background is straining and not fully legible. The website is easily accessible on various devices through being able to adjust to various screen sizes and the content will still be perceivable and operable. Keeping up with good website design techniques, Superbalist’s website can be interpreted by screen readers allowing further access to different individuals. The website also uses familiar signs and semantics that can help the user whilst exploring. Starting with symbols, the menu bar, search bar, and shopping cart icon are all easily identifiable by users allowing them to always understand the content shown at all times keeping these patterns constant throughout the site can also build an ability to be able to control how they interact with the site as they always know what to do with the element found on the site.

The website’s interactive design is pleasing as seen through its use of dropdowns. This feature is common in many websites and rightly so as it affords the user more knowledge and information and keeps a minimal look only serving to show the pages a user can explore and nothing else. The use of high-quality imagery is aesthetically pleasing as it gives the website a clean professional look to its products. The use of both a navigation bar (header) and a search bar allows the user multiple ways to find what they are looking for in the website limiting the possibility of being lost.

An adequately built UX system can help a user accomplish their goals. This is mostly through influencing and manipulating the user’s experiences and emotions and as Hassenzahl (2006) stated Experiences have advantages. The following are a few laws to achieve a healthy user experience and how Superbalist compares in each regard. The Locality laws state that elements should be placed in areas where they will influence change, if an element causes a change across an area, it should be placed above the affected area and finally, if an element affects change far from the area it should stand out. The Superbalist website does not fully follow any of the locality laws. The left sliding menu is affected button by a top left-placed menu bar button and buttons that lead you to specific pages are placed after the preview of the page (page titles are usually found underneath pictures that relate to the page). There is also a lack of “stand-out” elements which gives balance to the whole page as nothing is given more weight than its counterparts. Drawing on standing out gives rise to Van Restorffs effect of things in a crowd that stand out and are more remembered. As mentioned before few elements are given more weight than their counterparts and this leads to the page relying on other methods of relaying importance. This brings about a problem when you consider Hicks Law on how the time taken to make a decision increases as the quantity and the complexity of the choices increase. The Superbalist page has numerous subpages, making the home page elements hold equal weight, which makes it harder for a user, who does not know what they want when first visiting the page, to choose what to do next. There are a lot of pages to explore and a lot of content to take in so at face value this may be a lot for the user to process making it not effective for a first-time user to traverse through the site. The serial position effect does deal with a few of the aforementioned problems regarding an overload of information as this law states the first and last items in a series are remembered by the user and Superbalist takes advantage of this by making the featured style/collaboration of the day be the two popular figures in the creative industry (Daliwonga and Nqobile Khwezi). Making this the first section of the website keeps users drawn in as these are people of interest. Almost all the elements found on the website are buttons of different shapes and forms and this stylistic choice also affects the user’s experience. The use of square buttons (in the form of images) is common in corporate and fashion websites, and this could be because they can give a sense of containment and discipline giving a professional setting and setting a tone of seriousness. Circular buttons are also present on the website contradicting the already-established professional mood created as these styles of buttons are more playful and endearing. They are minimally used to not cause a shift in the tone of the website and can be interpreted as giving relief to the serious nature being enforced throughout the website.

Some ethics have to be considered when viewing the user experiences created by interacting with the website as a user’s experience is being manipulated in ways more than simply making them feel serious because the website is serious. The first issue is that of the inequalities presented on the website. One can argue that given the website originates from South Africa it would be designed in a manner that is aware of social contexts in South Africa. South Africans spend on average around 5% on clothing and footwear. This may not be alarming at first glance however for a poor country with individuals that do not have that much disposable income this figure can be considered too high. The reason for such high spending could be through websites such as Superbalist that offer constant discounts on products which act as incentives to users to always spend more on things they may not necessarily need. Digital inequalities mirror existing social inequalities (Robinson, 2015) and this is prevalent in this website as South Africans are not money savvy in the real world having only a 0.3% savings rate on their income. Companies are aware of this problem however they aim to see users spend more on their advertised products without any regard for consumer social statuses and Superbalist has reaped the benefits of this having made over $75m (R1,463b) in sales. This brings rise to the more pressing matter of being able to influence user behaviour. This can be done a step further by the external website factors such as the newsletter and email ads always promoting discounts and sales prevalent on the website. The use of a user’s personal information to bombard them with ads and newsletter updates is an ethical issue on two levels, causing a distraction in the user’s life and neglecting privacy (through always sending users updates and needing the user’s information in the first place). This can be taken a step further to being seen as exploiting the user for the sake of selling them products. The website is thus lacking in terms of being cordial to website design ethics and this is a common downfall faced by such websites as they always tend to value the company’s values more than the user’s needs.

Using the stylistic, UI, UX and interactive design elements essential for designing a well-functioning website Superbalist’s website can be deemed to be an effectively designed website. For the business, it communicates its products well and has efficient functionality however consumers are provided with more than enough information however they may also be overloaded with knowledge and nudged into making involuntary decisions. It is an efficient website but improvements can and need to be made.
